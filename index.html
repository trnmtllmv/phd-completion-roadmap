Thesis narrative

Thesis Story

A supervisor-facing narrative of the thesis argument, chapter logic, novelty and evidence discipline.

What the thesis says

The thesis argues that crude-oil biodegradation in coastal sands cannot be interpreted from microbial capability alone. Biological removal depends on whether oil, water, oxygen, nutrients and microbial cells occupy connected and accessible regions of the pore space. Therefore, laboratory biodegradation rates, pore-scale observations, column endpoints and field monitoring trends must be treated as different evidence classes rather than interchangeable measurements.

Central scientific problem

Hydrocarbon disappearance does not automatically equal biodegradation. It may also reflect evaporation, dissolution, redistribution, capillary trapping, physical loss, or model interpretation. The thesis therefore develops an evidence-based scale-transfer framework.

Chapter Storyline

Chapter 2 — Scientific gap

Crude oil biodegradation, coastal porous media, multiphase flow, monitoring and digital decision-support concepts.

Chapter 3 — Oil, markers and strains

Defines crude oil substrates, hydrocarbon markers and microbial strain basis, including reference strains and the local Newburgh isolate Streptomyces TM1.

Chapter 4 — Batch biodegradation

Tests strain-specific biodegradation behaviour under controlled liquid-phase conditions and identifies oxygen availability as a major operational control.

Chapter 5 — Sand-column platform

Characterises Newburgh fine sand, pore architecture and capillary-active behaviour as the porous-media test bed.

Chapter 6 — Pore-scale visualisation

Uses microfluidics to show how saturation state changes oil–microbe contact and phase redistribution.

Chapter 7 — Sand-column biodegradation

Tests static, neap and spring water-table forcing and evaluates physical loss, biological descriptors and depth-resolved TPH profiles.

Chapter 8 — ADR modelling

Interprets measured column endpoints using advection-dispersion-reaction modelling, rate-transfer checks and oxygen-demand constraints.

Chapter 9 — Field application

Applies the evidence chain to a Field A recovery emulator for offshore asset-retirement interpretation, while separating measured evidence, interpreted evidence and scenario projection.

Chapter 10 — Contribution and future work

Summarises the research questions, main contributions, limitations and future evidence needed.

Main novelties

Explicit multi-scale evidence chain

The thesis links microbial capability, oil chemistry, pore-scale accessibility, sand-column transport limitation, ADR interpretation and field-scale response planning.

Saturation state as a mechanistic control

The work shows that saturation state affects not only apparent oil removal rate but also the mechanism of oil–microbe contact.

Batch-to-column transfer constraint

The thesis shows that batch biodegradation descriptors cannot be transferred directly to column-scale porous media without accounting for transport, oxygen delivery and accessibility.

Newburgh fine sand as a coastal porous-media test bed

The sand-column work links crude oil retention, capillary-held water, hydraulic forcing and biodegradation response in a coastal sand system.

Local Streptomyces TM1 contribution

The thesis identifies and uses Streptomyces TM1 as a locally isolated hydrocarbon-degrading actinobacterium from Newburgh coastal sediment.

Microbial references in the thesis include Neptunomonas naphthovorans NAG-2N-126, Oleispira antarctica RB-8T and Streptomyces TM1.
